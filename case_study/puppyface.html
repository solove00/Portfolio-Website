Taking the work out of finding play:
A simplified park locator for families with children

THE PROBLEM

Even though there are many pet services apps in the marketplace, many of the current service providers are expensive and can’t meet pet owner’s requirements for cost and service. Rover is the most significant pet boarding app right now. But many pet sitters on Rover are asking for expensive service fees regardless of their service quality. Pet service fee is much higher for holidays. Traveling out of town during holidays become more difficult when you have to consider the cost, and worry about your pet’s stay at somebody’s house.

THE SOLUTION

PuppyFace solves the problems of expensive and unreliable pet services by building a credit based service exchange system between pet owners. With PuppyFace, pet owners can specify their preference for a boarding place, pet sitter’s experience, then book a service using credits (PuppyFace will give every new user ten free credits to start with). And at the same time, pet owners can also receive and check service requests from other pet owners. If the service request is accepted and finished, then the pet owner will gain credits and can be used for future pet service for his/her pets. Since the platform is not money involved and pet owners provide service to each other, PuppyFace is a more reliable and low-cost tool to find pet service.

MY ROLE

I functioned as the UX researcher, UX designer, visual designer for this project. Within these roles, I conducted user research, created wireframes, conducted user interview, and completed visual designs.

THE RESEARCH

User Research

I designed a user research survey to know my target users. My questionnaire focused on:

  • What people do with their pets when they travel;
  • Where do they find daycare or boarding places for their pets;
  • What concerns do they have for pet daycare/boarding;
  • For the places your pets usually go to boarding/daycare, what do they think they can improve;
  • Do they like to try a new app that can help them find free and reliable pet daycare/boarding.

Results were gathered from 12 respondents across the country.

I summarized the data and figured out pet owner’s motivation, frustration, and concerns:

  • Pet owners usually put their pets in boarding or someone’s home when they travel;
  • Pet owners find pet boarding places through the internet;
  • Safety, cost, experience were the most important considerations;
  • They would like the pets boarding places they usually send their pets can lower the price;
  • 61% of respondents liked to try a new app that can help them find FREE pet daycare/boarding.

It is clear that from these survey results pet owners wanted a search tool that can find reliable and low-cost pet services.

Personas

I conducted one-on-one interviews with three of my survey respondents to find out more details about their responses and help us understand the problem better. From these interviews, I developed three personas and identified there are three major user groups:

  • Caro represents pet owners who live in small towns where limited pet hotels are available;
  • Tara represents pet owners who have limited incomes like students, they prefer cheaper cost for pet services;
  • Max represents pet owners who live in a costly area like San Francisco, they like the idea of credit exchange for pet services.

The demand for reliable and cheaper pet service is popular. I also identified some features for implementation in the future development process, such as adding filters for senior dog experience, puppy experience, rescue dog experience, etc.

Competitive Analysis

After having a comprehensive understanding of my target audience and what they need, I start to do SWOT analysis for three popular pet service apps (Rover, Pawshake, PetBacker) in the market. I made a feature listof these three apps comparing all their features. And I examined their UI/UX designs in details by testing out these apps myself and reading app reviews.

User Stories

Based on all the surveys, interviews and competitive analysis, I came up with a list of user stories and solutions to meet the needs of pet owners.

What pet owners want Solutions
Reliability Services are provided by other pet owners, and they have experience and love for pets, not provided by profitable organizations, users can rate service providers
Low cost Services are exchanged using credits, not money which helps to lower household cost. Pet owners can provide services to gain credits and use credits for their own pets.
Filters for senior dog experience, puppy experience, rescue dog experience etc These options will be available in the filter because they are important to many pet owners. So that they find out if a pet sitter has required skills easily.

BRANDING

Many logos of pet’s brands can be divided into two groups: (1) colorful pet image to display a happy pet (2) one color strokes to present a feeling of clean and simple. From my research, I concluded that this product needed to get a balance between these two. Because the cuteness of pets can attract users, and the cleanness and simple can make users feel reliable.

Typography

Righteous is an open sans typeface. Its round bowl, counter, and aperture create a feeling of decor and cuteness. It is suitable for headlines and reminds people of the cuteness of puppies.

Gudea’s sans serif displays a simple, condensed structure that brings a pleasant feeling when used at any size. Gudea was initially used in engineering documents. It also provides a sense of reliability.

Brand Story & Logo

The product’s central premise was to locate outdoor play spaces that were close by, safe, and enjoyable for children, not unlike the backyard of one’s home. The word “backyard” also came up in several conversations between parents that I witnessed which inspired this project - how, given today’s housing market, families often cannot afford homes with a yard for kids to roam and explore, but they still want to encourage their kids to play in nature and learn to socialize; or how parents often came across public playgrounds near their homes on random walks that they hadn’t realized existed there. I wanted to capture the feelings of proximity and exploration in an open, natural, familial space, and “Backyard” was thereby born.

From there, Backyard needed a logo to communicate the product’s function, key message, and personality. The logo is a simple line illustration: the leaf-cum-magnifying glass conveys the product’s simplicity with some whimsy.

Colors

Backyard also needed a color palette to support feelings of serenity while evoking typical associations with the outdoors. The color inspiration came from a wondrous view of green grass meeting blue sky as I sat on a park bench during some informal field research in my neighborhood.

EXECUTION

Backyard transformed from a paper prototype to a clickable prototype through rounds of exploring visual design, conducting usability testing, and iterating on feedback.

Paper Prototype

I began with a pencil and paper to quickly sketch a low-fidelity prototype, focusing on providing opportunities to quickly narrow down search results and on minimizing the complexities of pinpointing family-oriented details. Rather than reinventing the wheel, I conducted multiple pattern analyses to extrapolate key elements of search, filter, view, and edit flows that met my criteria for convenience, speed, and organized simplicity.

Using paper prototypes allowed me to draw up several concept alternatives for the search, view, and edit screens, upon which I conducted basic user testing and preference tests. Feedback pointed me towards navigation and content organization as the top user priorities to keep in mind as I turned to Sketch to begin creating medium fidelity wireframes.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

I wanted to test which search view users would gravitate towards, and which categories of amenities users would click through. I also wanted to see how users would navigate through the entire process from search to review. I recruited participants to be recorded in in-person usability tests as well as a video recording through TryMyUI. In-person participants were guided through a clickable InVision prototype using a test script developed from the foundations provided by Steve Krug.

Feedback generated from the tests of my grayscale prototype centered on a few key takeaways, which informed my top action items:

  • Refine and build filter categories into an onboarding process to highlight the product’s kid-friendly purpose;
  • Clarify distinction between informative and action elements on selected park screen; and
  • Incorporate a feed to emphasize differences between bookmarking and checking in, re-format for consistency, and make adjustments to the main navigation accordingly.

High-Fidelity Prototype

Backyard came to life as I added color and iterated on my design to create a high fidelity, clickable InVision prototype, which I then used to conduct a second round of usability testing.

Using feedback from six participants in my second round of usability tests, I refined my prototype once again:

  • I improved existing coach marks explaining the bookmark and add visit functions given users’ difficulties in understanding and exiting the instructive modal.
  • I revisited the Add a Visit flow, incorporating buttons for users to leave a review or correct information so as to highlight the calls to action and reduce barriers to completing these tasks.
  • Because time and accuracy were equally important to users, I overhauled the Review screen after exploring how to generate accurate ratings using yes/no questions, a checklist, or behavioral data gathered as a whole.

KEY LEARNINGS

In creating this mobile application, I was challenged by the breadth and depth of information that users wanted for a product that would work like Google and Yelp, but better. From my research, current competitors appear to have conflicting brand identities and have not fully utilized data gathering and user testing to appeal to a broader demographic.

Working on this project also taught me the importance of organizing content to fit the needs of the specific user. Convenience and speed were real demands born out of parents’ demanding real-world needs, and every little detail that can shred seconds off completing a task is crucial.

With more time, I would continue to iterate on the user Profile screen to enable Backyard to further develop its social networking capabilities and build a foundation for potential e-commerce partnerships.
